An admissions or application essay, sometimes also called a personal statement or a statement of purpose, is an essay or other written statement written by an applicant, often a prospective student applying to some college, university, or graduate school. The application essay is a common part of the university and college admissions process. Some applications may require one or more essays to be completed, while others make essays optional or supplementary. Essay topics range from very specific to open-ended.

Applicaction Essays

Because the application essay can have a critical effect upon your progress toward a career, you should spend significantly more time, thought, and effort on it than its typically brief length would suggest.

Annotation Bibliographies

Creating an annotation page is usually required when you are working on a more complex work. For example, a simple essay will not require you to include annotations, but if you are writing a dissertation, research or course work on serious subjects like Medicine, History, Economics and other – you will have to include this page. Why? Annotation is intended to explain to the reader why chosen sources are relevant and important to your work.

STRUCTURE OF ANNOTATIONS

All students know that different types of work have quite similar structure. No matter if you have to create abstracts, research works, descriptive or ACT essays, the structure will be almost the same:

  • - Introduction – a short paragraph that introduces readers to the topic and contains a thesis statement and hypothesis.
  • - Main part. The main body of any text usually consists of several parts. Each paragraph of this section must present an argument that supports your hypothesis as stated in the intro, explains the problem and offers solutions.
  • - Conclusion. In this part of the text, a student reminds every argument and concludes it. The main goal of this section is to highlight critical messages and draw one or a few logical conclusions that confirm or deny your thesis statement.

JOURNAL ARTICLE DEVELOPMENT

When majority of students are faced with an assignment to write a review of an article, there are several reasons why it instantly becomes challenging and stressful. There are numerous questions that require answers like not knowing whether review is a summary or critique or what crucial elements should be included in paper's structure.

First of all, we hurry to answer that famous questions of many college students about what an article review is: a summary or critique. A student has to provide a summary of contents and evaluate what is written. As a rule, most university professors ask to work with research done by an expert in particular field, so there is usually more than enough argumentation or scientific terms to analyze.

What makes reviewing different from other essay types is a way how summary is put together so that it is linked to evaluation part. From main theme, arguments to critical flaws and calls for additional research, it has to be present. This is exactly what makes it an article review - a summarized analysis and opinion, based on existing strengths and weaknesses.

Article Review Format Peculiarities

In our guide, we will review basic requirements for MLA and APA formats.

  • - MLA Format: Below usual MLA header with your information, come up with a good title that reflects main idea of a review. It can be declarative, reflective, descriptive or even interrogative type of a title. Next, introduce article's author, specify where and when it has been published.

  • - APA Format: The main differences in APA format:
    Abstract of about 200-300 words contains summary of review with study conclusions; No references are cited in abstract; Abstract includes 4-6 keywords on topic;

Report Writng and Review

In academia there is some overlap between reports and essays, and the two words are sometimes used interchangeably, but reports are more likely to be needed for business, scientific and technical subjects, and in the workplace.

Essentially, a report is a short, sharp, concise document which is written for a particular purpose and audience. It generally sets outs and analyses a situation or problem, often making recommendations for future action. It is a factual paper, and needs to be clear and well-structured.

Requirements for the precise form and content of a report will vary between organisation and departments and in study between courses, from tutor to tutor, as well as between subjects, so it’s worth finding out if there are any specific guidelines before you start.

Reports VS Essays

Whereas an essay presents arguments and reasoning, a report concentrates on facts.

Reports may contain some or all of the following elements:

  • - A description of a sequence of events or a situation.
  • - Some interpretation of the significance of these events or situation, whether solely your own analysis or informed by the views of others, always carefully referenced of course
  • - An evaluation of the facts or the results of your research
  • - Discussion of the likely outcomes of future courses of action
  • - Your recommendations as to a course of action and Conclusions.
